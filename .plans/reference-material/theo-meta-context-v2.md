# Theo grounding: why role theater is legacy software

The argument Theo makes about Jira and Linear is really an argument about where process comes from and what happens when the constraints that created it disappear. Issue trackers weren't badly designed — they were correctly designed for a world where engineering time was genuinely scarce, humans were specialized into narrow roles, and coordination between those roles required ceremony to be reliable. A PM scoped work, an engineer picked it up, a reviewer approved it, a QA lead verified it. The process existed to bridge the gaps between people who could only do one of those things. The overhead was the coordination cost, and it was real.

The problem is that systems optimized for coordination costs tend to accumulate ceremony over time. More fields, more workflows, more stages, more required handoffs — each added for a real reason, but compounding until process becomes the work itself. Theo describes this directly from his time at Twitch: a Jira dashboard that took two minutes to load because every feature had been used and abused until it broke. The system wasn't wrong, it was just fully realized. That's what happens when you optimize hard for a constraint and then the constraint goes away.

There's also a second reason ceremony persists beyond pure inertia: it's legible to promotion systems. Theo notes that his prototype-first method actually hurt his career growth at Twitch because there were no large spec documents to point at during reviews. The specs weren't producing better software — but they were visible artifacts that signaled rigor to people evaluating his work. This is why process accumulates even when practitioners know it's hollow. The people who wrote the skills and rules being audited were trained in environments that rewarded visible artifacts, and those habits transfer directly into how they write prompts. Role theater isn't just cargo-culted thoughtlessness; it's often a rational response to incentive systems that reward the appearance of structure over its utility.

The constraint that's going away is role specialization. When Theo criticizes GStack — the system of AI personas like "the CEO," "the senior designer," "the QA lead" — his critique is that those boundaries existed because individual humans could only be one thing. Coordination between specialists was expensive, so you built systems to manage it. But if a model can do all of those things, preserving the org-chart structure is just theater. You're paying the coordination cost without getting the specialization benefit. The useful part of a "senior designer" persona isn't the identity, it's whatever actual procedure is smuggled inside the label: what to check, what constraints to apply, what good looks like. Strip the costume and you have an SOP. Keep the costume and you have overhead.

Theo's working method at Twitch anticipated this. Instead of writing a spec before building, he'd spend a few days on a rough prototype — not to ship it, but to find out what the real plan should be. The prototype exposed the technical surface area, the UX assumptions that were wrong, and the shortcuts that would actually work. Then he'd write a better spec, or more often just polish the prototype and ship it. His observation is that pre-written specs were almost always wrong because they were written before anyone understood the problem. The ceremony of spec-writing wasn't producing better outcomes; it was producing a paper trail that looked like rigor. The prototype was the plan, and everything written before it was prediction theater.

The other thing that changed is the cost of code itself. The old ceremonies were worth paying for when engineering time was genuinely scarce — protecting expensive human attention justified the overhead. That calculus has flipped. Code is now cheap enough that building a rough version to find out what you actually need costs less than writing the spec that will inevitably be wrong. When the thing being protected is no longer scarce, the protection racket stops making sense. Every planning ritual, persona wrapper, and multi-step review process inherited from the expensive-code era needs to justify itself against that new baseline.

This is the lens to use when auditing skills and rules: the question is not whether something sounds structured, but whether it reduces the distance between intent and execution. A checklist that catches real failure modes is valuable. A persona wrapper that makes an agent feel like a specialist is ceremony. Stage gates that encode actual quality bars are valuable. Multi-step planning rituals that produce artifacts no one reads are overhead. The test Theo would apply is blunt: if the role label disappeared, would the method still work? If yes, the label was never the point. If no, you've found something that actually matters — and you should write it down as a procedure, not as a character to play.

The deeper implication for agent systems specifically is that the old justification for role-based design — coordination between specialists is expensive — doesn't hold when the same system can do the whole workflow. What replaces it is context plus rules plus execution routing. You need to capture what's known, define what good looks like, specify when to escalate and to whom, and then let the model carry it to completion. That's the substrate underneath every role that was ever worth having. Everything else was scaffolding for a coordination problem that no longer exists.

One honest counterargument Theo raises, and then sets aside: maybe models actually perform better when organized around human structures, because they're trained on human-generated data. If enough of the training corpus shows a "senior designer" thinking in a particular way, maybe the persona activates useful behavior that a bare procedural prompt wouldn't. He doesn't dismiss this entirely — he calls it a genuine uncertainty. But his working bet is still that execution-first outperforms structure-for-its-own-sake, and the right posture when auditing is to be skeptical of identity layers while remaining open to keeping any structure that demonstrably improves output quality. The burden of proof belongs on the ceremony, not on the removal of it.

There's also a warning embedded in how Theo reads the current moment. He describes the MCP story as a dry run: the field convinced itself that a new standard for model-data access was the right abstraction, built a lot of infrastructure around it, discovered it was worse than just writing code, and eventually returned to code. His prediction is that agent process design is going through the same loop right now — rebuilding everything in human-shaped terms because that's the familiar frame, before eventually discovering that execution-first was right all along. The implication for this work is that the skills and rules repos being audited right now are probably full of that first-loop thinking. The role theater isn't ancient legacy; it was written recently, by people who were doing their best with the instincts they had. That's exactly why it needs auditing.
